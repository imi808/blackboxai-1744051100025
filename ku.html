Ku

God of war, politics, fishing and male productivity

Domain and Significance

Ku is one of the four principal Hawaiian deities, representing war, politics, fishing, and male productivity. He is often depicted as a fierce warrior god, though he has many manifestations with different specialties.

Ku was particularly important to Hawaiian chiefs and warriors. His worship involved strict kapu (taboos) and sometimes human sacrifice. Despite his warlike nature, Ku also represents male creative power and was invoked for successful fishing expeditions and canoe building.

Symbols and Representations

Natural Elements

  • Storms and strong winds
  • Sharks and other powerful sea creatures
  • Koa trees (used for weapons and canoes)
  • Volcanic stone (for tools and weapons)

Sacred Objects

  • War clubs (newa) and spears
  • Feathered capes and helmets
  • Wooden temple images (ki'i)
  • Fishhooks (makau)

Myths and Stories

Ku and the Creation of Canoes

Ku is credited with teaching Hawaiians how to build canoes. According to legend, he appeared as a stranger who helped a fisherman build the first outrigger canoe, revealing his divine nature only after the successful completion.

Ku and the Battle with the Mo'o

One famous story tells of Ku battling a powerful mo'o (dragon/lizard spirit) that was terrorizing a village. After a fierce battle, Ku transformed the mo'o into stone, creating a prominent rock formation that still exists today.

Ku's Seasonal Cycle

Ku's worship was seasonal, alternating with Lono. While Lono ruled during the peaceful Makahiki season, Ku's time began when the season ended, marked by the dismantling of Lono's banners and the resumption of warfare.
