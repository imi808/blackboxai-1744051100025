Pele

Goddess of volcanoes, fire, lightning, and dance

Domain and Significance

Pele is perhaps the most well-known Hawaiian deity, revered as the goddess of volcanoes and fire. She controls the lava flows that create and destroy land, embodying both creative and destructive forces.

Pele is also associated with lightning, wind, and hula (dance). She is considered a passionate and temperamental deity, capable of great generosity and terrible wrath. Many Hawaiians still honor Pele with offerings at volcanic sites.

Symbols and Representations

Natural Elements

  • Lava and volcanic eruptions
  • Fire and lightning
  • Volcanic rock formations
  • Steam vents and sulfur banks

Sacred Objects

  • Pāhoehoe (smooth lava)
  • ʻŌhiʻa lehua flowers
  • Red and yellow kapa cloth
  • Pele's hair (volcanic glass fibers)

Myths and Stories

Pele's Journey to Hawaii

According to legend, Pele traveled from Tahiti to Hawaii in search of a home, digging fire pits at various islands before settling in Kīlauea on the Big Island. Her angry sister, the sea goddess Namakaokaha'i, chased her, leading to epic battles between fire and water.

Pele and Lohi'au

One of the most famous stories tells of Pele's love for the mortal chief Lohi'au. This tragic romance is the basis for many hula dances and chants, including the famous "Hi'iakaikapoliopele" epic.

Modern Sightings

Many people claim to have seen Pele as a beautiful young woman or an old hag near volcanic areas. It's considered bad luck to take volcanic rocks from Hawaii, as Pele may curse those who steal from her domain.
